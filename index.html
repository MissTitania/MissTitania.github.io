Some older undertakings don't appear here. In high school I worked on a bridge bidding AI, a challenging problem thanks to imperfect information, cooperative agents, and ludicrously-high game complexity. To this day programs are inadequate for top-level competition. My algorithm relied on drawing random hands consistent with bids thus far for Monte Carlo simulation, recursively iterating (re-generating samples at each step) to foresee potential outcomes from both self and partner perspectives without ever acting as if under perfect information from either side. Outcomes were rated with a double-dummy solver. The result was powerful enough to consistently defeat random Bridge Base Online opponents! Another hobby project was the Netflix Contest, for which I made attempts myself and followed some of the published papers. Mostly these methods were based on stochastic gradient descent with feature extraction vectors using k-fold cross-valuation on training data. Admittedly out-performing Netflix's own rating prediction algorithm was trivial, but as you may infer I did not manage to best teams of PhDs to win the $1,000,000 (*Pout*).

This is an in-browser AI implementation for the classic game Othello. If you haven't played before, I recommend you start here with the rules.

This AI runs a minimax search with alpha-beta pruning heuristics to find the most favorable obtainable position. There's no transposition table because the collision rate in Othello is too low to be worthwhile. Mid-game states are evaluated using a function pre-computed through neural networks on such features as differential of legal moves and the perfect-play results of sub-games of different board subsets.

The neural nets are coded and trained separately in Java to allow for blazing-fast computation with bitboard representations. The technique stores boards as 2 64-bit integers (1 for each player) marking all piece positions with a 1 bit. With some cleverness, this allows all relevant features like legal moves, flipped pieces, and board subsets to be calculated in extremely few clock cycles with basic bitwise operations and precomputed masks.
